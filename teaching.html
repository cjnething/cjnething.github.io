Teaching

What years of teaching programming taught me about learning.

Teaching adults changing careers is one of the most rigorous tests of whether you actually understand something. It also taught me more about product design than most engineering roles ever did.

Bootcamp teaching & mentorship

As a Technical Mentor at Hack Reactor (2020–2022), I worked with adult learners in an intensive coding bootcamp—people changing careers, often with no prior programming background, learning under significant time pressure. I had significant freedom to design my own lessons and assignments, and I regularly used Google Form surveys to check whether new approaches were actually helping students learn.

Earlier roles at Hack Reactor and MakerSquare:

  • Hacker in Residence (2015) — Lectures on React Native, algorithms, Git, and Chrome DevTools; admissions technical interviews
  • Hack Reactor Prep — Co-teacher and teaching assistant for beginning programming (HTML/CSS, JavaScript, Git, algorithms)
  • MakerPrep / MakerSquare (Austin, 2016) — Co-taught intro programming, preparing students for immersive bootcamps

Topics across these roles:

  • JavaScript fundamentals and asynchronous programming
  • React and component-based UI development
  • Node.js, SQL, MongoDB, and full-stack development
  • Debugging strategies and problem decomposition
  • Git, functional programming, and computer science fundamentals

A React lesson that mostly failed

One lesson I was particularly excited about tried to teach React by building the same interface three ways: vanilla JavaScript, jQuery, and React. My hypothesis was that seeing the progression would help students understand why React existed and what problems it solved.

The result surprised me. The overwhelming majority of students found the lesson more confusing than helpful. But a small number came to me afterward and said it was the first explanation of React that had ever made the concept click.

In retrospect, I think I made a bad assumption: I treated vanilla JS and jQuery as existing knowledge when many students were still actively learning those. Instead of comparing three approaches, a lot of students felt like they were learning three new things at once.

That experience changed how I think about teaching. A lesson can be ineffective on average and still provide a breakthrough for a subset of learners—and different people often need completely different entry points into the same concept. I became less interested in whether something worked in general, and more interested in who it worked for and why.

Teaching adults changing careers

One lesson from teaching is that learners are rarely struggling with the same thing. Two people can appear equally stuck while needing completely different explanations.

Bootcamp students aren't traditional undergraduates. They're often highly motivated, deeply anxious, and carrying years of professional experience in other fields. This creates a specific set of learning dynamics:

  • Imposter syndrome is structural, not personal. Adult learners often interpret normal difficulty as evidence they don't belong. The job is to normalize struggle without lowering standards.
  • Misconceptions are predictable. The same wrong mental models appear again and again—especially around async code, scope, and recursion. Good teaching targets misconceptions directly, not just syntax.
  • Confidence and competence develop on different timelines. A student can feel capable before they are, or feel lost while actually progressing. Both need different interventions.
  • Breaking down difficult concepts is a design problem. How you sequence material, what examples you choose, and when you introduce abstraction all determine whether something clicks or doesn't.

What This Means for Product

Teaching gave me a practitioner's understanding of skill acquisition—not from reading about it, but from watching hundreds of people try to learn the same hard things and seeing what helped and what didn't.
