Teaching

What years of teaching programming taught me about learning.

Teaching adults changing careers is one of the most rigorous tests of whether you actually understand something. It also taught me more about product design than most engineering roles ever did.

Teaching adults changing careers

Bootcamp students aren't traditional undergraduates. They're often highly motivated, deeply anxious, and carrying years of professional experience in other fields. This creates a specific set of learning dynamics:

  • Imposter syndrome is structural, not personal. Adult learners often interpret normal difficulty as evidence they don't belong. The job is to normalize struggle without lowering standards.
  • Misconceptions are predictable. The same wrong mental models appear again and again—especially around async code, scope, and recursion. Good teaching targets misconceptions directly, not just syntax.
  • Confidence and competence develop on different timelines. A student can feel capable before they are, or feel lost while actually progressing. Both need different interventions.
  • Breaking down difficult concepts is a design problem. How you sequence material, what examples you choose, and when you introduce abstraction all determine whether something clicks or doesn't.

Teaching gave me a practitioner's understanding of skill acquisition—not from reading about it, but from watching hundreds of people try to learn the same hard things and seeing what helped and what didn't.
