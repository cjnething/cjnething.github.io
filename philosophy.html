Philosophy

What I believe about learning and technology.

Technology should expand what people can do—not replace the doing. The best tools make you more capable over time, not more dependent.

Capability over engagement

I'm skeptical of products optimized for time-on-site, completion rates, or streaks. These metrics tell you whether someone showed up—not whether they can actually do something new afterward.

The question I ask about any educational or AI tool is: after using this, is the person genuinely more capable? Can they solve a problem they couldn't before? Do they understand something they didn't? That's the metric that matters.

Maintaining a 2,000+ day Duolingo streak while realizing I wasn't making meaningful progress in the language made this concrete for me personally. A tool can bring someone back every day and still fail to make them more capable—and the user can know that and show up anyway. How I think about that →

Teaching revealed this

Years of teaching programming to adult learners made this concrete. You watch someone struggle with recursion not because they're incapable, but because the concept requires a mental model that almost nothing in everyday experience prepares you for.

Good teaching—and good tools—meet people where their mental models actually are, not where you wish they were. They target misconceptions directly. They give learners enough structure to build confidence without removing the productive struggle that actually builds skill.

The goal isn't to make learning feel easy. It's to make learning work.

Tools should help people think

The best developer tools, creative tools, and learning platforms don't do the thinking for you—they extend your reach. They remove friction that doesn't teach anything while preserving friction that does.

This is especially important for AI. An AI assistant that writes code you don't understand isn't making you more capable—it's making you dependent. An AI partner that helps you reason through a problem, surfaces relevant concepts, and adapts to what you already know? That's augmentation.

AI as augmentation, not replacement

I don't think AI should replace human judgment, creativity, or the hard work of developing expertise. I think it should change what's possible for people who are actively trying to learn and build.

The design challenge isn't "how do we get users to use AI more?" It's "how do we design interactions where AI use correlates with genuine capability growth?" That requires understanding learning, not just language models.
