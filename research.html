Research Software

Building tools for researchers, alongside researchers.

Research software engineering is a distinct skill: building tools that let domain experts do their jobs—self-service infrastructure, validation systems, data pipelines—not imposing engineering assumptions on research problems.

RAND Corporation

At RAND Corporation (Nov 2018–Nov 2019), I worked as a full-stack research software engineer. My work focused on building software that let researchers do their jobs more effectively—from self-service cloud deployment to custom validation tools and data collection pipelines—not on shipping generic internal apps.

The through-line was working with people whose expertise is something other than software engineering: collaborating with domain experts, iterating when requirements were ambiguous, and refining systems based on how researchers actually used them.

Stack: React, JavaScript, Django, Python, Scrapy, AWS, Docker, Git.

Self-service AWS deployment

I built an internal tool that let researchers provision cloud infrastructure on AWS without a manual handoff to ops. Before that, standing up an environment meant waiting on someone else; after, researchers could do it themselves when they needed to run something.

That's a developer-tools story in a research context: removing friction that didn't teach anyone anything, so experts could spend their time on the work they were actually there to do.

Puerto Rico disaster recovery dashboard

One project supported researchers in Puerto Rico doing cost-estimation validation for hurricane recovery work after Hurricanes Irma and Maria. The team needed to track a large volume of estimates, documents, and validation status across many recovery activities—not a generic file manager, but a dashboard shaped around their actual workflow.

This work involved:

  • Designing a dashboard for tracking validation progress across recovery cost estimates
  • Building file upload and organization features tailored to researcher workflows
  • Collaborating with the Puerto Rico team to iterate on features and validation logic—not just taking initial specs and disappearing
  • Supporting accuracy and reproducibility in a high-stakes policy context

Background on the broader recovery effort: RAND's Puerto Rico disaster recovery planning project.

Dark web research support

I also built a Scrapy-based pipeline in Python to scrape and analyze dark web product listings—open-ended data collection to support research initiatives researchers couldn't easily do themselves. Another example of the same pattern: researchers with a question, software as the tool to help them investigate it.

Survey recruitment — pro-Ukraine influencers

A separate project, co-authored as RR-4429: RAND researchers used targeted Twitter advertising to recruit pro-Ukraine influencers, pro-Russia activists, and a general Russian-language audience in Ukraine for survey research on countering Russian information operations.

My contribution was hands-on with Twitter—setting up ad campaigns to drive survey participation and working with tweet data to support the research team's analysis. A lot of the work was figuring out what the platform actually allowed: research goals vs. Twitter's terms of service don't always line up, and someone on the engineering side has to push back when a request crosses that line.

Why this matters

Many software engineers have startup or product company experience. Fewer have spent significant time building software in research environments—where users are experts in their domain but not in yours, where requirements are discovered through collaboration rather than specified upfront, and where the cost of getting something wrong is measured in research quality, not just user retention.

I keep ending up in that situation: RAND researchers, Hack Reactor students, Capital One internal teams, Thorn data scientists. The job is rarely "build what the ticket says." It's figure out what non-engineers need to be more effective, and build that.

This experience shaped how I approach user research, requirements gathering, and the gap between what people say they need and what actually helps them work. It's the same muscle that HCI research develops formally—and research software develops through practice.
