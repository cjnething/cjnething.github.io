AI

Intelligent systems in service of human capability.

My interest in AI isn't about model performance benchmarks or training pipelines. It's about how intelligent systems can be designed to help people learn, reason, and build real skill—and I've been building toward that question through coursework and professional work for years, not since ChatGPT launched.

OMSCS — AI & Intelligent Systems

My graduate work at Georgia Tech included substantial AI coursework through the Interactive Intelligence specialization (now Artificial Intelligence):

  • Artificial Intelligence — search algorithms, adversarial game playing, constraint satisfaction, and planning under uncertainty
  • Knowledge-Based AI — cognitive systems, knowledge representation, reasoning, and case-based reasoning. This course bridged AI architectures and human cognition—how expertise can be represented and supported, not just pattern-matched.
  • AI for Robotics — intelligent agents perceiving and acting in physical environments—planning, decision-making, and embodied intelligence
  • Machine Learning for Trading — applied ML for decision-making under uncertainty and time pressure

This gives legitimate AI grounding without positioning the work as ML research. The Education Labs role isn't "train a better model"—it's "build products that help humans become more capable using AI." Having enough AI depth to understand the space while remaining focused on humans is the right balance.

Connected to human learning

The AI coursework doesn't stand alone. It sits alongside a cluster focused on human learning and capability:

  • Educational Technology — how interventions affect learning outcomes
  • Human-Computer Interaction — how people actually interact with systems
  • Data & Visual Analytics — how information should be presented so people can understand complex concepts, build mental models, and avoid cognitive overload. See Municipal Bond Pricing.
  • Knowledge-Based AI — the bridge between both clusters

Knowledge-Based AI is the intellectual link: it treats intelligent systems as structured reasoning that can complement human thinking—not replace it. Data & Visual Analytics addresses a parallel question from the product side: how should complex information be shown so users actually understand it?

Human–AI collaboration

I'm interested in AI as a collaborative partner in learning and building—not a replacement for either. The design questions that matter to me:

  • When should an AI system provide an answer vs. prompt further reasoning?
  • How do you calibrate assistance to a user's current skill level?
  • How much information should be shown—and how should it be structured?
  • What interaction patterns build capability vs. create dependency?
  • How do you measure whether AI use correlates with genuine skill growth?

These aren't purely technical questions. They're learning science, HCI, and information design questions that happen to involve language models.

Educational applications

Teaching programming gave me a grounded view of where AI assistance helps and where it hurts. Students who copy AI-generated solutions without understanding them learn nothing. Students who use AI to explore alternatives, debug their thinking, or get unstuck on a specific conceptual blocker can learn faster—if the interaction is designed correctly.

Projects like Recursion Immersion represent the kind of intentional instructional design I think AI tools need more of: grounded in learning science, evaluated on outcomes, and built to develop mental models rather than produce outputs.

Production AI systems

At Thorn, I work on large-scale Python classification services for text, image, and video—production ML infrastructure where reliability, latency, and responsible deployment matter. Recent work includes resolving OOM failures (61% memory reduction), event-driven pipelines with Kafka and Pub/Sub, and CI improvements that make releases safer. It's a different domain from EdTech, but the same design tension: automated systems that must support human judgment, not replace it.

Current practice

I use AI tools actively in my own engineering and research work—and pay attention to how they change my own capability over time. Which uses make me better at my job? Which uses atrophy skills I want to maintain? That lived experience informs how I think about designing AI features for others.
