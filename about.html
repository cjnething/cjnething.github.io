About

Building software, teaching people, studying how people learn.

My career sits at an unusual intersection: production engineering, classroom teaching, graduate research in HCI and learning science, and research software for domain experts. These aren't separate chapters—they're an evidence trail showing why I ended up interested in AI-assisted learning and human capability.

Where it started

My undergraduate honors thesis examined cyberattacks against Estonia and Georgia, and the process of researching it ultimately led me into computer science. As an undergraduate at UNC Chapel Hill, I was pursuing a degree in Peace, War and Defense (Honors, with Distinction) and a minor in Russian culture—I wanted a thesis topic at the intersection of conflict and institutions, Russian history and perspective, and whatever was actually happening on the technical side.

A faculty advisor suggested that if I really wanted to understand that last part, I should take a computer science course. One course became a minor, then Hack Reactor, and eventually a career. OMSCS came later, while working. I didn't set out to become a software engineer; I set out to answer a question, and programming turned out to be useful—and then its own thing.

That's a pattern I notice in myself: when a question interests me, my response usually isn't "that's outside my field." It's "guess I need to learn a new field." The disciplines are tools I pick up along the way. Questions I'm thinking about →

A recurring theme: human capability

I didn't accidentally take one HCI class or one educational technology course. Over years of engineering, teaching, and graduate study, I kept gravitating toward the same questions:

  • How do people learn difficult concepts?
  • How do people interact with technology?
  • How do intelligent systems reason—and how can they support human reasoning?
  • How should information be presented so people can actually understand it?
  • How do we design experiences that develop expertise over time?

That trajectory connects Hack Reactor mentorship, RAND research software, OMSCS coursework, and projects like Recursion Immersion and the Meetup HCI redesign. It's a coherent path—not a CS degree with random electives.

Research software

Research software engineering is one of the through-lines of my career—not a detour from product work. At RAND Corporation, I built tools that let researchers do their jobs without waiting on engineers: self-service AWS deployment, a cost-estimation validation dashboard for Puerto Rico hurricane recovery, dark web data collection pipelines, and hands-on support for co-authored survey research.
