Municipal Bond Pricing

A team project combining machine learning and interactive visualization to help muni bond traders sift through 260,000+ bonds and quickly identify pricing outliers—reducing information asymmetry in an opaque, manually priced market.

Team #153 · Georgia Tech OMSCS · Data & Visual Analytics

Quick links Paper Demo video Poster

Overview

Municipal bonds are debt securities issued by states, municipalities, and counties to finance capital expenditures. They benefit issuers through lower funding costs and investors through tax-free income—but tax implications, credit risk, liquidity, and dozens of other interdependent factors make pricing complex and inefficient.

This project was completed as part of Georgia Tech's Data & Visual Analytics course by Team #153 (2Legit2Quit): Jacob Burbank-Goldrich, Janarthanan Gopi, Issac J Kuo, Saswata Misra, Constance Julia Nething, and Angela Tran.

Problem

Muni bond pricing is opaque and inefficient, driven by high complexity and unequal information between parties. Pricing is done manually by a handful of experts. Prior research has explored data-driven approaches—ridge regression, neural networks, support vector regression—but these haven't broadly taken hold, and none incorporated a configurable visual layer to help traders refine their analysis on a case-by-case basis.

Objective

Build a tool that helps muni bond traders identify outliers in their existing pricing scheme—bonds priced too high (may never sell) or too low (profit not maximized)—so they can price inventory appropriately and trade more efficiently.

Approach

Our innovation was combining a machine learning pricing baseline with a configurable visualization layer—allowing traders to compare their bonds against comparable bonds, reduce research time, and increase transparency in the market.

  • Use ML to establish a pricing baseline, then identify outliers relative to that baseline
  • Display similar bonds in a configurable visualization with associated metrics
  • Enable drill-down from high-level geographic patterns to individual bond details

Data & modeling

We acquired three populations of U.S. municipal bonds as of September 2020, totaling 272,283 records—historical trades from MSRB, competing bonds from Bloomberg Runz, and current inventory from a team member's firm. After enrichment with Bloomberg security-level data and cleaning (excluding infeasible spreads), we analyzed 266,837 records across 17 attributes.

We evaluated multiple clustering algorithms (K-means, PCA + DBSCAN, Kamila, FAMD) and regression approaches. Clustering did not produce sufficiently accurate groupings for the business purpose. After systematic model comparison, an Adaboost Decision Tree Regressor was selected as the final model (MSE 0.0239, RMSE 0.1546)—outperforming linear regression, Random Forest, and cluster-based regression approaches.

The model predicted spreads on the inventory population, and we computed a relative "sigma" score—(actual spread − model spread) / standard deviation—measuring how far each bond's pricing deviates from the model baseline relative to expected noise.

Tools: Python (pandas, matplotlib, scikit-learn) for data cleaning, exploratory analysis, and modeling; R for additional clustering experiments (K-means, Kamila, FAMD).

Visualization & interface

The UI was designed to help traders navigate complexity without drowning in data:

  • Overview: A choropleth map of the U.S. color-coded by outlier count per state, paired with a sortable table of states and outlier scores—interconnected so selecting a state highlights the corresponding row
  • Drill-down: Right-clicking a state opens a scatterplot of bonds (outlier score on x-axis, spread on y-axis) with a ranked bond list and hover/click details
  • Filters: Additional controls to narrow bonds displayed on the scatterplot

Color coding made actionable insights immediately visible: green for bonds priced too high (spread too low, may not sell), red for bonds priced too low (spread too high, gains not maximized), and blue for outlier historical trades that might signal future investment opportunities.

The core design challenge: enable a user to sift through 260,000+ bonds and quickly surface the handful worth investigating—presenting enough context to support a decision without overwhelming cognitive load.

Outcomes

The tool successfully demonstrated pricing opportunities across the inventory population—bonds that should be priced lower to increase sale probability, and bonds that should be priced higher to maximize profitability. An unanticipated benefit: visualizing outliers in the historical population helped identify patterns in past trades that might represent future investment opportunities.
