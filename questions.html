Questions

Things I keep thinking about.

The rest of this site is evidence. This page is curiosity—the questions I actually care about, including ones I don't have answers to yet.

Learning & capability

  • How can AI help people build expertise rather than dependence?
  • Why are some concepts disproportionately difficult to learn—and why does the same concept break differently for different people?
  • What makes a tool capability-building rather than merely convenient?
  • How should we measure learning when the easy metrics are all engagement?
  • When should a system give an answer vs. prompt further reasoning?
  • What does it take for a difficult concept to actually stick—not just make sense in the moment?

Teaching difficult things

Recursion is the obvious example—I spent a semester building software around it—but the pattern shows up everywhere. Some ideas have a gulf between "I follow the explanation" and "I could do this myself." I want to understand that gulf better.

  • Why do some students need the physical puzzle before the code, and others need it the other way around?
  • What makes a metaphor helpful vs. misleading?
  • How do you teach something you learned so long ago you've forgotten what was hard about it?

Engagement vs. learning

I have a complicated relationship with Duolingo. I like it—I really do. I also have a 2,110-day streak, and for the past year or so I've been gaming it without making much meaningful progress in the language. I know I'm doing that. I know it's not ideal. And yet I haven't stopped—partly because the streak keeps me in the game, and partly because the owl has me hooked. I know Duo's disappointed face is engineered guilt. I open the app anyway.

That's what makes it interesting to me—not the number. Duolingo is clearly succeeding at something: I've come back every day for years. But if the goal is "learn Russian," the picture gets murkier. What does success even mean for a learning tool—daily return, or actual capability?

I'm not being tricked. I can see exactly what the streak is doing. It still works on me anyway. That gap between engagement and learning is something I think about a lot—especially for AI tutors that might optimize for usage the same way. More in Philosophy.

Rabbit holes

I tend to become fascinated by systems that most people take for granted—especially ones that seem almost magical until you understand how they work. Looking back, a lot of my rabbit holes turn out to be about perception and interpretation—how people observe, categorize, and make sense of what they're seeing—though I didn't realize that until I started writing this page.

Recently I've spent a lot of time reading about olfaction: how noses convert trace molecules into information, why animals can track scents that humans can't detect, and whether it's possible to build sensors that approach the capabilities of a bloodhound. The question that keeps pulling me in isn't just how smell works—it's what kinds of information exist that humans simply aren't equipped to perceive. Could we build something that finds missing people, detects disease, or reads an environment the way a dog does?

Russian literature has been a long-running interest since my undergraduate minor in Russian culture—not because it's prestigious, but because it's weird. One author I keep returning to is Fyodor Sologub, whose work is equal parts bizarre, funny, and unsettling. There's a scene where a character sees an unfamiliar plant and immediately concludes the only explanation is that it's poisonous and his friend is trying to kill him. I find that kind of logic delightful—absurd, dark, and weirdly insightful. I once wrote a paper arguing that many of his short stories can be understood through the Fall from Grace narrative. What pulls me in isn't really "Russian literature" as a category; it's how people receive information and construct completely bonkers explanations for it.

Lately I've become fascinated by personal color analysis—the whole world of self-proclaimed experts who confidently tell people they're a Summer or an Autumn, and the fact that two experts can look at the same person and reach completely opposite conclusions. That raised a question I couldn't let go of: wait, then what are they measuring? It sits in an uncomfortable space between aesthetics, psychology, and expertise—how much of color perception is biological versus cultural, which claims are actually measurable, and how much of what we call expertise is pattern recognition versus subjective interpretation?

Smaller detours happen too—like learning how the Kia Center converts between hockey, basketball, concerts, and ice shows after an event—but the ones above are the ones I keep coming back to.

I don't know where most of these questions lead. I just enjoy following them long enough to understand how things work.

Where the answers might live

Some of these questions show up in my projects, philosophy, and writing. Most don't have a deliverable yet—they're just the things my brain does on a Tuesday evening.
